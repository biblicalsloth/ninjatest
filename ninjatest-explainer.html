00 — The one-minute version

What Ninjatest actually is

Ninjatest is a 1-versus-1 battle game for people studying for the CAT exam (the entrance test for India's top business schools). Two students are matched against each other and answer the same 9 questions — 3 reading, 3 logic/data, 3 maths — under a countdown clock, at the same time.

Whoever scores more wins, and — like chess ratings — each player carries a number that goes up when they win and down when they lose. Because both players face identical questions on a synchronized timer, and because the referee lives on our servers rather than on the player's phone, nobody can cheat their way to a better score.

Around that core battle sits everything you'd expect from a real product: profiles and stats, a leaderboard, monthly seasons, skill-tier "leagues", win streaks, friends and private messages, friendly challenge links, a spectator mode to watch live games, an AI tutor called Ninja that explains any question, and an admin console for managing the question bank.

In plain terms

Think of it as competitive online chess, but the "moves" are CAT questions — with a global scoreboard, ranked seasons, and a coach on the side.

One thing to know today

The full game is built and battle-tested but currently hidden. A setting called "waitlist mode" is switched on, so the public website only shows a marketing + sign-up page right now. Flip one setting and the real game becomes the front door. This is a deliberate go-to-market choice, not a bug.

01 — The big picture

Three layers, one game

Every app like this is really three pieces working together. Picture a board game: there's the board and pieces you touch, the rulebook that says what's legal, and the notepad where the score is written down. Ninjatest has exactly those three.

1 · The screen (what players see)

The app on the player's phone or laptop. It shows the questions, the countdown, the buttons to tap. It is deliberately "dumb" about scoring — it never decides who won. It just draws the game and sends the player's taps onward.

↕ questions go down · answers go up
2 · The brain (the rulebook & referee)

A set of trusted programs on our servers that do all the real thinking: match players fairly, pick questions, time the clock, score answers, calculate ratings, catch cheating. This is the only place decisions are made.

↕ reads & writes the record
3 · The memory (the permanent record)

A database — think of a giant, locked set of spreadsheets — that remembers everything: every player, every match, every answer, every rating change, the whole question bank. Nothing is forgotten, and players can only read the rows they're allowed to.

In plain terms

The golden rule of the whole system: never trust the screen. The player's device can be tampered with, so the brain re-checks and re-calculates everything itself. The screen is a window, not a judge.

The tools underneath (named once, then forgotten)

You don't need these, but if an engineer mentions them: the screen is built with Next.js and React (popular website-building tools) and lives on Vercel (a hosting company). The brain and memory are both provided by Supabase (a company that bundles a database, logins, and a real-time messaging system). The AI tutor calls out to OpenAI-style models (like the ones behind ChatGPT). That's the entire toolbox.

02 — A player's journey

From "never heard of it" to "ranked #1"

Here is the whole product as a story, in the order a real person experiences it.

  • Discover & join the waitlistToday the first thing a visitor sees is the marketing landing page. They fill out a short 6-step survey and leave their email to join the waitlist. (When the game goes live, this page instead sends logged-in players straight to the lobby.)
  • Sign up & onboardingNew players create an account with email + password or a one-tap Google sign-in. A brand-new player is walked through a quick 3-step setup: their name and username, which exam they're preparing for, and their target year.
  • The lobbyHome base. Shows their rating, rank, streak, daily tasks, who's online, and a big button to start a battle.
  • The queueThey tap "find a match" and wait a few seconds while the system looks for an opponent of similar skill. A small heartbeat keeps their spot alive; if nobody suitable is nearby, the net widens.
  • The matchThe two players are locked in. A shared 3-second countdown starts them together, then question 1 appears with its own timer. Answer, wait for the reveal, next question — nine times. A live indicator shows when the opponent has answered (but never what they answered).
  • The resultThe final scores, who won, and exactly how many rating points changed hands. A full answer review shows every question, the correct choice, and an explanation.
  • Profile, stats & leaderboardTheir public profile tracks rating over time, win/loss record, per-subject strengths (charts), and match history. The global leaderboard ranks the top 100.
  • Seasons, leagues & streaksRatings feed into skill tiers (Bronze up to Diamond) and monthly seasons that reset gently. Winning builds a streak; losing resets it.
  • Social playAdd friends, send direct messages, and challenge a friend to a private match with a shareable link. Watch other people's live matches as a spectator.
  • Get help from NinjaAfter a question is done, the player can ask Ninja, the built-in AI tutor, to walk through how to solve it.
Why this shape matters

Solo exam prep is lonely and hard to measure. Turning practice into ranked, social competition gives players a reason to come back daily, a way to see real improvement (the rating), and opponents at their level so games stay winnable. That's the entire retention thesis of the product.

03 — The brain, explained

Every rule of the game, without the code

This is the heart of the product — the trusted referee that runs on our servers. Each piece below follows the same six questions: what it is, why it exists, what it does, how it works, why it matters to the player, and any quick suggestion.

Matchmaking & the queue

What
The system that pairs two waiting players into a match.
Why
A game is only fun if your opponent is roughly your level. Too strong is demoralizing; too weak is boring.
What it does
When you ask for a match, you join a waiting list. The system looks for another waiter whose rating is close to yours. The longer you wait, the wider it's willing to search — starting near your rating and stretching out by up to a thousand points over time.
How
Your app sends a quiet "I'm still here" heartbeat every 20 seconds. A background job runs every minute to pair people and to clean out anyone whose heartbeat went silent. Two players can never grab the same opponent at once — the system locks each pairing so there are no double-bookings. There's also a cap of fewer than 3 ranked games against the same person per day, so friends can't farm easy points off each other.
For the player
Fast, fair matches against real peers — and protection from rating manipulation.

The fairness rule: "never trust the screen"

What
A design principle, not a feature — but it governs everything else.
Why
A determined cheater can edit what happens on their own device. If the device were allowed to report its own score, cheating would be trivial.
What it does
All scoring, timing, rating, and matchmaking decisions happen only on the server. The player's app can request things and show results, but it can never assert "I got 900 points" and be believed.
How
These trusted programs run with special privileges inside the database and re-derive every number from scratch. Even the clock is measured on the server, not the phone.
For the player
The ladder means something. A rating earned here reflects real skill, because the system is structurally cheat-resistant.

Choosing the 9 questions & adaptive difficulty

What
How each match's question set is picked, and how questions get quietly easier or harder over time.
Why
Questions should match the players' level, and the difficulty label on each question should reflect reality — not a guess made when it was written.
What it does
A standard match is 3 reading + 3 logic/data + 3 maths, aimed near the two players' average skill with a bit of randomness so games aren't repetitive. Reading and logic questions prefer to pull a whole passage group (several sub-questions sharing one passage). Section-mode challenges instead serve 9 questions from a single subject.
How
Every question carries its own "rating", just like a player. When someone answers it in a ranked game, the question's rating nudges up or down depending on whether people find it easy or hard — so the bank self-calibrates. (Unranked games never nudge it, because that was once a loophole to quietly weaken hard questions.) Suspiciously fast answers are ignored so bots can't skew it.
For the player
Questions feel appropriately challenging, and the difficulty system keeps improving on its own.

Scoring an answer

What
How points are awarded for each of the 9 questions.
Why
Rewarding both accuracy and speed makes the game tense and skill-expressive.
What it does
A correct answer earns a base of 100 points plus a speed bonus — the faster you answer, the bigger the bonus. A wrong answer loses 30 points. Skipping earns zero. Maths and logic questions carry a higher speed multiplier than reading.
How
The server measures how long you actually took using its own clock, caps it at the question's time limit, and computes the bonus in fixed "blocks" of saved time. Every scoring dial (base points, penalty, time limits) lives in a settings table — never hard-coded — so they can be tuned without a rebuild.
For the player
Being fast and right is rewarded; reckless guessing is punished. Skill shows.

The rating system (how you climb)

What
The chess-style number that goes up when you win and down when you lose. Everyone starts at 1000.
Why
It's the single measure of skill that drives matchmaking, leagues, and the leaderboard — and the reason to keep playing.
What it does
After a ranked match, the winner gains points and the loser loses the same number (strictly zero-sum). How many depends on the rating gap (beating someone stronger is worth more), how new you are (newcomers move faster), and how decisive the win was (a blowout moves the rating more than a nail-biter). There's a floor of 100 so nobody can be driven into the ground, and streaks are tracked for wins.
How
At the moment of finalizing, the server reads both players' current ratings under a careful lock, so two of your games finishing at once can't corrupt each other. Draws split cleanly with no free points. Unranked and no-skill games move nothing.
For the player
A rating that's fair, tamper-proof, and genuinely reflects who they can beat.

Timers & the synchronized clock

What
The per-question countdown, kept honest and identical for both players.
Why
Everyone's device clock is slightly wrong. If timing were trusted to the phone, players could gain seconds.
What it does
Each subject has its own limit — reading 90 seconds, maths 105, logic 120. A shared 3-second lead-in starts both players together. When time hits zero, an answer is auto-submitted for you.
How
The server stamps when each question started. Your app measures its own clock's error against the server once at match start and draws the countdown against the corrected time. But the real elapsed time that scores you is always measured server-side.
For the player
A perfectly fair, identical clock — no advantage from a fast or slow device.

Forfeits, no-shows & disconnections

What
What happens when an opponent vanishes mid-game or never shows up.
Why
You shouldn't be stuck waiting forever, and quitters shouldn't dodge losses — but a brief internet blip shouldn't cost you the game either.
What it does
If your opponent disappears, the app waits a short grace period, then lets you claim the win. A background job also sweeps up games where someone never answered.
How
A forfeit is only granted with server-verified proof of absence — the opponent genuinely missed a full question deadline with no response. No-skill forfeits transfer no rating. This proof requirement is what stops people from faking a win by claiming their opponent "left".
For the player
Rage-quitters can't escape, honest players aren't punished for a dropped connection.

Spectating (watch live matches)

What
A read-only mode to watch other people's games in progress.
Why
Watching top players is fun, aspirational, and good for community.
What it does
Browse a list of live matches and drop into any one to follow along.
How
Spectators receive a carefully filtered feed — only safe information, never anything that could leak answers to the players. Crucially, spectators are invisible to the game: their presence (or leaving) can never affect a forfeit.
For the player
Learn from better players; adds a spectator-sport dimension.

Seasons & leagues

What
Recurring competitive periods, and skill tiers from Bronze to Diamond.
Why
A fresh season gives everyone a periodic reason to climb again; leagues give a friendly badge that's easier to grasp than a raw number.
What it does
Each season ends on a schedule, snapshots the standings, and applies a gentle reset that pulls everyone halfway back toward the middle (so leaders keep an edge but the field bunches up). Leagues are just rating bands: Bronze, Silver (1200+), Gold (1500+), Platinum (1800+), Diamond (2100+).
How
An hourly background job handles the season rollover automatically. Leagues aren't stored anywhere — they're computed on the fly from the rating you already have.
For the player
Long-term goals, seasonal fresh starts, and a clear badge of where they stand.

Friends, messages & challenge links

What
A social layer: friend requests, private direct messages, and personal battle invites.
Why
Playing with people you know is stickier than playing strangers, and messaging keeps the rivalry alive between games.
What it does
Search for players, send/accept friend requests, remove friends, exchange direct messages, and send a friend a private challenge link (or aim a challenge straight at their in-app inbox). Unread messages show a red count.
How
Messages arrive instantly through the live channel. You can only message people who've accepted your friendship, message length is capped, and there's a rate limit so nobody can spam. Reading history is allowed only for the two people in a conversation; sending goes through the trusted server so authorship can't be faked.
For the player
A reason to bring friends in, and to keep coming back to talk trash and rematch.

Onboarding (first-run setup)

What
A short 3-step wizard shown only to brand-new players.
Why
Capturing name, target exam, and target year personalizes the experience and gives you useful signup data.
What it does
Step 1 name + username, step 2 which exam (CAT, XAT, and a few others), step 3 target year.
How
The server validates everything (username characters, length, allowed exams, sensible year) and flags the account as "onboarded" so it's never shown again. Existing users were quietly marked complete so only genuinely new people see it.
For the player
A friendly, guided start instead of being dumped into an empty app.

Ninja — the AI tutor

What
A built-in AI coach that explains how to solve any question the player has already faced.
Why
The learning happens after the battle. An on-demand explanation turns a loss into a lesson.
What it does
A floating "Ninja" button; when a player taps a finished question, Ninja writes a step-by-step worked solution ending in a clear "Answer:" line, and keeps a history of past explanations per question.
How
The request goes to our server, which checks the player was actually in that match and the question is finished (so it can't be used to peek at a live answer), then sends the question — plus the correct answer and official explanation, which never touch the player's screen — to an AI model so it can explain accurately. The model, prompt, and on/off switch are all controlled from the admin console with no code change or redeploy. It's rate-limited to prevent runaway costs.
For the player
An always-available tutor that turns every question into a teachable moment.

Email notifications

What
Automated emails for challenges and match results.
Why
Nudges bring people back and make challenges feel real.
What it does
Sends a challenge invite and a results summary.
How
Each email endpoint carefully verifies the sender is allowed — you can only email about a challenge you own or a match you played, and only to your own address. (An earlier, looser version could have been abused to send mail to anyone; that hole was closed.)
For the player
Timely, trustworthy nudges — never spam.

Admin console (question bank management)

What
A private control panel for the team to manage questions and settings.
Why
The content is the product; the team needs to add, edit, and toggle questions without touching code.
What it does
Bulk-upload questions (as a file or spreadsheet), group passages, turn questions on/off, flag under-stocked subjects, view the waitlist, and configure the Ninja AI tutor.
How
Every admin action first checks the caller is a verified admin. Access is granted deliberately by the team, never automatically at signup.
For the player
Indirect but vital — a fresh, well-curated question bank.

Rate limiting (anti-abuse)

What
Caps on how often any action can be performed.
Why
Stops spam, brute-force abuse, and runaway costs.
What it does
Limits things like answer submissions, joining the queue, messaging, and AI requests — both per person and per internet address.
How
Two independent layers, both stored durably (so a restart doesn't reset them). The per-address layer fails open by design — if the limiter itself hiccups, real users aren't locked out.
For the player
A stable, spam-free app that stays affordable to run.
In plain terms

Everything above lives as small, trusted programs (about 45 of them) inside the database. The player's app just asks — "match me", "score this", "who won?" — and the server does the thinking and answers. That separation is the whole reason the game is fair.

04 — The screen, explained

What players see — and how it stays alive

The screens themselves

What
The visible pages: landing/waitlist, sign-up & login, onboarding, lobby, queue, the live match, the result, profiles, leaderboard, spectate, settings, friends, challenge-accept, and the admin console.
Why
Each is one clear step in the journey; nothing extra to get lost in.
What it does
Draws the current state and forwards the player's taps to the brain.
How
Public pages (leaderboard, profiles) are pre-built and cached so they load instantly for logged-out visitors and cost little to serve; personal pages are built fresh each visit.
For the player
Fast pages and a focused, uncluttered flow.

Staying live without refreshing

What
The match, queue, and chat update themselves in real time.
Why
A battle where you had to hit "refresh" would be unplayable.
What it does
The moment your opponent answers, the question advances, a message arrives, or a match is found, your screen reacts on its own.
How
The app keeps a live connection open to the server. Two kinds of signal travel it: "liveness" pings (like "opponent answered" — never the actual answer) and authoritative state (the real score, pulled from the record). The record is always the source of truth.
For the player
A smooth, instant, real-time feel.

Surviving a dropped connection

What
Graceful recovery when Wi-Fi blips mid-match.
Why
Real internet is unreliable; a blip shouldn't lose a game.
What it does
When the connection returns, the app quietly re-reads the true state from the server and carries on where things really stand.
How
On any error or when the browser comes back online, it "rehydrates" from the database rather than trusting whatever was on screen.
For the player
Resilience — brief drops don't ruin the match.

The look & feel

What
A dark, focused, arcade-meets-terminal aesthetic.
Why
Dark reduces eye strain in long study sessions and reads as serious and competitive.
What it does
Near-black violet background, one mint-green accent for actions, gold for ratings, pink for losses, monospace type throughout.
How
Dark mode is intentionally the only mode. A consistent set of colors and one font give it a distinctive, coherent identity.
For the player
A calm, confident, recognizable brand.

05 — The memory, explained

The spreadsheets the app never forgets

The database is a set of locked, related "spreadsheets" (called tables). Here are the important ones in plain terms.

The spreadsheetWhat it remembers
PlayersEach person: rating, peak rating, wins/losses/draws, current & best streak, and whether they're an admin.
MatchesEvery game: who played, the 9 frozen questions, current question, running scores, and the ratings before & after.
AnswersOne row per player per question — what they picked and how long they took.
QuestionsThe question bank: text, options, the correct answer, its live difficulty rating, and how often it's been seen. Players can never read this directly.
QueueWho's currently waiting to be matched.
Rating historyEvery rating change over time — this powers the profile graph.
Seasons & resultsPast and current competitive periods and their final standings.
Friends & messagesWho's friends with whom, and every direct message.
ChallengesPrivate invite links and whom they're aimed at.
WaitlistMarketing sign-ups and their survey answers.
Settings & safety logsAll the tunable scoring dials, the AI config, and behind-the-scenes anti-cheat & rate-limit records.
In plain terms

Each spreadsheet has a locked door (a rule called "row-level security") that controls who may read which rows. You can read your own matches and messages; you can never read the answer key or someone else's private chat. Sensitive tables have no player door at all — only the trusted server programs can open them.

06 — Fair & safe

Why cheating is genuinely hard here

Competitive integrity was clearly a first-class concern. Here's the honest, reassuring version of how it's protected.

  • The server is the only judge. Scores, timing, and ratings are all computed on our servers. The player's device is never believed.
  • Answers are hidden and shuffled. The correct answer is never sent to the screen during play, and each player sees the answer options in a different order — so you can't tell a friend "tap the third one".
  • The clock is measured server-side. A fast or hacked device clock earns no extra time.
  • Rating farming is capped. Only a few ranked games against the same person per day count, and no-skill forfeits transfer nothing.
  • Suspiciously fast answers are flagged and excluded from difficulty calculations, blunting bots.
  • Spam and abuse are rate-limited on two independent layers.
  • Emails can't be hijacked — every send checks you're actually allowed to send it, only to yourself.
  • Locked doors on the data. Every table enforces who can read what; the answer key and private chats are sealed off from players entirely.
Why it matters

The rating is the whole point of the product. If it could be gamed, the leaderboard would be meaningless and serious students would leave. This much rigor is exactly right for a competitive product — and it's already been through three-plus rounds of security hardening.

Honest look at what's missing or fragile

09 — Plain-English glossary

Every technical word, defined once

Backend / "the brain"
The trusted programs running on our servers that make all the real decisions. Players never see it.
Frontend / "the screen"
The app on the player's device — the part they see and tap. It shows things but decides nothing important.
Database / "the memory"
A giant, locked set of related spreadsheets that permanently remembers everything.
Table
One spreadsheet inside the database (e.g. "Players", "Matches").
Server function (RPC)
A single trusted program in the brain that does one job — "score this answer", "match me". There are about 45.
Migration
A recorded change to the database's structure. Ninjatest has 56, forming a full history of how the memory was shaped.
ELO / rating
The chess-style skill number that rises with wins and falls with losses. Everyone starts at 1000.
Matchmaking
The process of pairing two similarly-skilled players into a game.
Queue
The waiting line you join while the system finds you an opponent.
Real-time / live connection
An always-open line to the server so screens update instantly, without refreshing.
Row-level security (RLS)
The "locked door" rule on each spreadsheet that controls exactly which rows a person may read.
Rate limiting
Caps on how often an action can happen, to stop spam and abuse.
Cron job
A task the server runs automatically on a schedule (e.g. matchmaking sweeps every minute).
OAuth (Google sign-in)
Logging in with an existing Google account instead of a new password.
Caching / ISR
Serving a pre-built copy of a page so it loads instantly and cheaply.
Waitlist mode
A setting that hides the game and shows only the marketing sign-up page. Currently switched on.
Fails open
A safety choice: if the anti-abuse limiter itself breaks, real users are let through rather than locked out.
Zero-sum
The winner gains exactly the points the loser loses — no rating is created or destroyed.
